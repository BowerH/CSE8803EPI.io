Social Determinants of Methicillin-Resistant Staphylococcus aureus Bloodstream Infection Patterns in California

We integrated eight years of California MRSA surveillance with county-level SDOH data and found that MRSA risk is driven primarily by population scale, healthcare utilization, and prior-year infections, with only limited additional predictive value from most socioeconomic indicators.

Authors: Hannah Bower, Alan Wang, AJ Subudhi  ·  Georgia Institute of Technology

Project Overview

Summary

Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) remains a major antimicrobial-resistant threat in the United States, yet the extent to which local socioeconomic and structural factors influence county-level infection burden is poorly understood.

In this project, we link eight years of California MRSA bloodstream infection surveillance (2017 - 2024) with county-level Social Determinants of Health (SDOH) indicators and hospital patient-day data. Our goal is to quantify how population scale, healthcare utilization, and community conditions shape MRSA risk across counties and hospital types, and to evaluate whether SDOH improve prediction beyond known exposure and population factors.

We compare linear models, time series baselines, tree-based ensembles, boosting, and deep learning approaches to forecast county-level MRSA burden and interpret the resulting feature importance patterns.

Methods

Data. We assembled a county–year panel for 2017–2024 by merging:

  • Hospital-reported MRSA bloodstream infections for each California county.
  • County-level SDOH indicators (income, housing, education, insurance, environmental metrics).
  • Patient-days by hospital type (e.g., Major Teaching, Community, Long-Term Acute Care).

Targets. We modeled both:

  • Total MRSA infections per county year.
  • Facility-specific infections stratified by nine hospital categories.

Features. Temporal (year), geographic identifiers (county/state), hospital patient-days, SDOH variables, and one-year lagged infection counts for each county and hospital type.

Models evaluated.

  • Linear Regression (with and without SDOH).
  • Univariate ARIMA time-series baselines per county.
  • Random Forest (Base, +SDOH, +SDOH+Lag).
  • XGBoost Regressor (+SDOH+Lag).
  • Feed-forward neural network (+SDOH+Lag).

Models were trained on 2017–2023 and evaluated on 2024 using MAE, RMSE, and R². For the best Random Forest configuration, we performed hyperparameter tuning and rolling-origin cross-validation to assess temporal robustness.

Key Results

Model performance

Across all models, tree-based methods were most robust to multicollinearity and nonlinear relationships. The tuned Random Forest with SDOH + lag features was the best-performing model on the 2024 test set, achieving:

  • RMSE = 9.02
  • MAE = 5.4
  • R² = 0.924

Linear models degraded sharply when SDOH were added due to extreme multicollinearity. XGBoost performed moderately well but was more sensitive to noise in year to year fluctuations. The neural network severely overfit the small, highly correlated dataset (R² < 0), confirming that deep learning was poorly matched to the available data.

ARIMA provided strong fits for individual county time series but could not incorporate cross-county structure, hospital-type exposure, or SDOH, making it unsuitable as a unified forecasting framework.

Epidemiologic insights

Hospital-type patterns.

  • Highest predictability in Major Teaching hospitals (R² ≈ 0.93) and Long-Term Acute Care facilities (R² ≈ 0.90), where high-acuity patients and long stays support steady, repeatable MRSA transmission.
  • Moderate performance in Community hospitals, with variability concentrated in medium volume facilities.
  • Low R² in low volume settings (Critical Access, Free-Standing Rehabilitation, Pediatric) despite tiny absolute errors, reflecting inherently sporadic, stochastic infections.

Geographic distribution.

  • MRSA burden is concentrated in a small set of large, urban counties (e.g., Los Angeles, San Diego, Sacramento, Orange).
  • These counties show strong temporal persistence: high burden areas remain high year-to-year.
  • Most rural counties report zero or near zero infections, producing noisy, hard topredict patterns.
Feature importance

The tuned Random Forest revealed a clear hierarchy of predictors:

  • Population scale: labor force, population, and population rank were the most influential features.
  • Healthcare exposure: patient-days in Major Teaching, Community, Pediatric, and Rehabilitation hospitals captured the intensity of inpatient contact networks.
  • Temporal persistence: lagged infections in Major Teaching and Community Large hospitals were among the top predictors, highlighting strong autoregressive structure.
  • SDOH: only a few structural indicators—especially % Severe Housing Problems and Income Ratio—added consistent signal; most other SDOH variables contributed little once population and patient-days were included.

Overall, MRSA risk at the county level is driven primarily by population size, hospital utilization, and previous-year infections, with SDOH playing a secondary and highly correlated role.

Conclusion & Future Work

Our findings show that county-level MRSA patterns in California are governed mainly by three forces: population scale, healthcare exposure, and temporal persistence of infections. The tuned Random Forest with SDOH and lagged features consistently outperformed linear, boosting, deep learning, and univariate time-series approaches, and its feature importance profile aligns closely with known epidemiology of MRSA bloodstream infections.

Social Determinants of Health, while often central in qualitative discussions of MRSA risk, contributed only limited incremental predictive value beyond population and patient-day measures. A small subset of structural indicators—especially severe housing problems and income inequality—remained important, suggesting that crowding and socioeconomic stressors do modulate risk but are largely captured through exposure proxies.

Future work will extend this framework to generate 2025 forecasts, explore multi-year lag structures and smoothed SDOH trajectories, and integrate these predictions into targeted surveillance tools for high-volume hospital environments and structurally disadvantaged communities.
